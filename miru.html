2nd Place HackWashington 2019

Introduction

The second hackathon I attended took place at the Seattle Space Needle! My team decided on making a social media app for the blind. I was in charge of the design and also worked on the front-end using HTML, CSS, and React.js. My team consisted of Chayanne, Abhinav, and Nicholas.

The Problem

Social media is all about connecting with others, sharing content, and discovering what’s happening nearby. However, it is very hard for the visually impaired to use social platforms especially when most social media apps are so visual.

The Solution

Miru is a social media platform for the blind and its features makes it super accessible for the visually impaired.

Name

I discovered from my teammate that translating any word to Japanese will usually make a good app name. Therefore, I came up with the name Miru, which literally translates to "see" in Japanese. Our app, Miru, aims to help the blind "see" the world.

Login Page

You can start the app using voice. All the functionality in Miru is activated using voice, and the feedback is also in audio-form, which means it’s accessible for the visually impaired.

Two Options

The first option is to navigate to a destination, in which you'll say "navigate". If you want to take a photo first, you can say "camera" to open the camera.

Navigate

Once your destination is chosen, miru will direct you (using voice) to the location. Upon doing research online, we discovered that it's difficult for the blind to discover new places. They have apps to direct them to the goal destination, but they often miss out on the interesting landmarks they're passing without even realizing. Miru will parse information about landmarks online and as you pass by the place, it'll read out a description.

Photo blurry?

Once you arrive at the destination, you can choose to take a photo. Because the blind can't tell whether the photo is good, our image detection functionality will actually tell you if the photo is blurry. If it is, you can try again.

Photo Options

If the photo is successfully taken, Azure's computer vision will describe the photo to us. The descriptions are very accurate. However, once we share the picture to our network, our friends can choose to help us out and send us a more detailed description, or make a comment. Each time we share a photo, it will tag the location and as more users use the app, there will be more places of interest added to the list.

End Note

Miru makes discovering new places and seeing the world easier for the visually impaired.
